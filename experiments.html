1) Nanoparticle Core Synthesis

  • Theory
  • Protocol
  • Result

Aim

To synthesize accurate solid silica nanoparticles required for making the Biosensor container.

Background

Monodisperse particles of silica are formed by Stober's process. The process consist of addition of silicate ester in excess of water containing a lower molar-mass of alcohol and ammonia. Size of silica nanoparticle formed depends upon the type of silicate ester used, alcohol and volume ratio. Tetraethyl orthosilicate(TEOS) is used as silicate ester and alcohol is ethanol. TEOS is hydrolysed by slightly basic solution to give silicon tetra hydroxide which on polycondensation give silica. LaMer and Dinegar model explains the nucleation and growth of nanoparticles. Particle diameter is also dependent on the temperature.

  • In our first experiment we created our solid silica nanoparticles of the specific size needed (around 150 nm) using the standard Stober's process.
  • We tried ultra-sonication for different times and selected the best conditions out of all which would lead to minimal agglomeration of the particles.

Preparation of solid silica nanoparticles

  • 35.7 mL of absolute ethanol was mixed with 5 mL water and 0.8 mL of ammonia and stirred for 5–10 minutes at room temperature in falcon tube.
  • Then 1 mL of TEOS was added and the mixture was allowed to react in different conditions to standardize the protocol. The different conditions were:
    1. Stirred at room temperature for 1 h.
    2. Non-stirred at room temperature for 1 h.
    3. Stirred at room temperature for 30 min.
    4. Non-stirred at room temperature for 30 min.
  • Afterward, dSiO2 nanoparticles was washed with water and ethanol and centrifuged at 12000 rpm for 10 minutes.
  • The particles were then suspended in 10 mL of water (1X solution).

Ultra-sonication of the solid silica nanoparticles

  • We created 10X diluted solution for 10 different time conditions - 20 sec, 40 sec, 60 sec, 120 sec, 200 sec, 300 sec.
  • We then conducted sonication for respective time frames and four different conditions.

2) Mesoporous layer formation

  • Theory
  • Protocol
  • Result

Aim

To coat mesoporous silica layer on the top of our already formed solid silica nanoparticles:

  • optimize the reaction conditions to get the best product
  • and determine the size of the overall nanoparticle

Background

TEOS, Cetrimonium Bromide (CTAB) and TriEthanolAmine (TEA) are used for preparation of mesoporous silica layer over these solid silica nanoparticle (dSiO2). TEOS is the silica source, CTAB (a cationic surfactant) is the structure directing agent , and TEA is co-structure directing agent. This procedure will lead to the formation of dSiO2(@CTAB/MSN).

  • We mixed 2g of CTAB (cationic surfactant) and 20 mg of TEA in 20 ml of mili Q water.
  • The mixture was stirred at room temperature for 1 hour.
  • 7.5 ml of 1X solid silica nanoparticle (SiO2) solution was added to the above mixture and stirred the mixture at room temperature for 1 hour again.
  • We added 0.15 ml of Tetra Ethyl Orthosilicate (TEOS) to the mixture.
  • This mixture is stirred in two different conditions to standardize the protocol.
    1. 80 °C and 1 hour.
    2. 25 °C and 20 hour.

We found that 25 °C and 20 hours reaction condition gave us the best results. Following are the TEM images showing the prepared SiO2@MSN/CTAB (solid silica nanoparticles with mesoporous layer and CTAB in the pores).

Fig 2. In the above figure, the mesoporous layer is clearly visible over the solid silica nanoparticle. The particles are of 220nm size

3) Etching: Hollow MSN formation

  • Theory
  • Protocol
  • Result

Aim

To etch out the solid silica nanoparticle from SiO2@MSN/CTAB to create a hollow space for our sensing substance (MSN@CTAB).

  • optimize the reaction conditions to get the best product
  • and determine the size of the overall nanoparticle

Background

We need to etch out the solid silica core template from dSiO2(@CTAB/MSN) to make hollow mesoporous silica nanoparticles (HMSNs). The silica core dissolves in alkaline conditions (pH 10.7). Na2CO3 is used to maintain the pH. The anionic silicates dissolve out of the solid core and are adsorbed at the CTA+ surface present on the mesoporous silica layer. The electrostatic interaction between CTA+ and silicate removes the latter from the solution as they continue to dissolve out and drives the reaction forward.

  • We added 21.2 mg/ml Na2CO3 in the SiO2@MSN/CTAB solution
  • The mixture is stirred at 50 °C for 30 minutes
  • This mixture is stirred in two different conditions for etching to standardize the protocol
    1. 80°C and 1 hour
    2. 25°C and 20 hours

4) HMSN surface blocking

  • Theory
  • Protocol
  • Result

Aim

To block the nanoparticle surface using GOPS.

Background

HMSN pores are blocked by CTAB and are the surface for gate attachment. However, to prevent the gate modules from binding to the surface, the surface is blocked before attaching gates.

  • Take the sonicated sample of nanoparticles
  • The solution is centrifuged and the nanoparticles are dried at 120 °C for 10 minutes.
  • 0.00374 ml GOPS is mixed in 1.7 ml of toluene to make a 10mM solution. GOPS reacts violently with polar solvents therefore the sample is dried to remove water and organic solvent such as dry toluene is used.
  • Then 20 mg dried nanoparticles are added to the above mixture.
  • The solution is left for 6-8 hours at 70 °C.

5) HMSN pore unblocking

  • Theory
  • Protocol

Aim

To remove CTAB from the pores of HMSN so that the sensing material can be entrapped in the particles

  • optimize the reaction conditions to get the best product
  • and determine the size of the overall nanoparticle

Background

The pores are blocked by CTAB after the formation Mesoporous silica layer. Pores need to be unblocked to fill the nanoparticles with signalling molecules and gate them.

  • The HMSN synthesized are dissolved in 80 ml acetone and refluxed at 80 °C for 48 hours.
  • This process (extraction process) is repeated 3 times to fully remove CTAB.
  • The final products are collected and washed with deionized water.

7) Synthesis of Gating module

  • Theory
  • Protocol
  • Result

Aim

Synthesize arsenic sensitive gating modules and test their function

Background

Aptamers specific to the element to be sensed base pairs with linker DNA molecules. The linker molecules are functionalized with GOPS. To demonstrate the functionality of the gates, our team designed gates sensitive to arsenic and also demonstrated their disintegration upon binding to arsenic.

Annealing Buffer:10 mM Tris, pH 7.5–8.0, 50 mM NaCl, 1 mM EDTA

We have to first attach the two linker DNAs functionalized with GOPS to our aptamer to make the gates. We need to anneal the linker DNA with the aptamer.

  • Resuspending the Oligonucleotides:
    1. Both linkers and aptamers are resuspended at the same molar concentration of 50uM, in Annealing Buffer.
  • Annealing the Oligonucleotides:
    1. 10ul each of both linkers and aptamer are mixed in a 1.5 ml microfuge tube.
    2. Tubes are placed in a standard heatblock at 90–95°C for 3–5 minutes.
    3. The heat block is removed from the apparatus and allowed to cool to room temperature (or at least below 30°C) on the workbench. Slow cooling to room temperature took 45–60 minutes.
  • Test gate opening with arsenic

    • 20 ul of 0.02 ppm arsenic was added to 10ul of annealed gate product and incubated for 1 hour.
    • Sample was then run on an agarose gel.

Following are the gel electrophoresis images for the result.

  • Lane 1 and 2 contain linker DNAs of 20bp each. Their position with respect to ladder proves their size.
  • Lane 3 contains aptamer DNA oligonucleotide of 100bp also is positioned correctly with respect to ladder.
  • Lane 4 contains the gate module mixture of the two linkers and the aptamer. Bright band position below the 50 bp mark of ladder explains a failed try to anneal the aptamer to the linkers.
  • Lane 5 contains the 50bp ladder
  • Lane 6 contains a fresh mixture of aptamer and linkers The bright band (~140bp or 80bp+20bp) positioned just above with respect to the 100bp band of ladder confirms successful annealing of the aptamers and the linkers to form the gate complex.
  • Lane 7 contains the mixture of the lane 4 gate complex and As(III). The bright band represents a failed reaction.
  • Lane 8 contains mixture of As(III) and lane 6 gate mixture. 2 bright bands are formed.
    1. Band near the 100bp mark represents the aptamer – As(III) complex
    2. Band formed below the 50bp mark represents the free linkers which have dissociated from the aptamer

Hence we successfully demonstrated synthesis of aptamer based gates and disintegration upon binding with respective ligand. However, the overall effeciency of the process and its universality (use with a variety of ligands) still needs to be improved and its universality needs to be confirmed by testing with multiple ligand.
